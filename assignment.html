Is Airbnb still a sharing economy?

Airbnb didn’t start as a global platform — it started with three guys in San Francisco who couldn’t pay their rent. In 2007, they decided to put a few air mattresses in their living room and rent them out to strangers attending a design conference. They called it “Air Bed & Breakfast”. But somewhere along the way, something changed. In cities like New York, Airbnb is no longer just about renting out a spare room for a few nights. Some hosts now manage multiple properties, operate year-round, and behave more like professional landlords than casual sharers. So the question is no longer how Airbnb works — but what it has become. Is Airbnb still a sharing economy… or something closer to a business?

Why this matters

Airbnb was originally built around the idea of home-sharing. But in a city like New York, that idea may coexist with something very different: hosts managing multiple listings, entire apartments offered as near full-time rentals, and spatial concentration in central areas. This matters because Airbnb does not just reflect cities — it can also reshape how housing is used.

Our central question is simple: does Airbnb in NYC still look like sharing, or does it increasingly look like business?

This analysis uses a February 2026 snapshot of New York City listings from Inside Airbnb, a project that collects publicly available Airbnb listing information for research and public discussion. It is a cross-sectional dataset, meaning it shows the platform at one point in time rather than over many years.

To answer our question, we move from market structure (who owns listings), to listing type (what is being rented), to behavior (how available listings are), and finally to space (where these patterns are concentrated in the city).

In other words, the goal is not just to describe Airbnb, but to test whether the patterns we see are more consistent with casual home-sharing or with a more business-driven platform.

Who really owns Airbnb listings?

A first way to test the “sharing economy” idea is to look at host concentration. If Airbnb mainly consists of casual hosts, most people should manage just one listing. If a smaller group controls many properties, the platform starts to look more like a market with professional operators.

Figure 1. Distribution of the number of listings per host in NYC. Takeaway: Most hosts manage only one listing, but a small minority controls many properties, suggesting that part of Airbnb activity is more professionalized than purely casual.

What are people actually renting?

Room type gives a second clue. If Airbnb mostly reflects classic home-sharing, we would expect many private rooms. If a large part of the market consists of entire homes or apartments, that points toward a model that is closer to full-unit rental activity.

Figure 2. Distribution of Airbnb room types in NYC. Takeaway: Private rooms remain common, but the large presence of entire-home rentals shows that Airbnb goes beyond the original idea of simply sharing spare space.
Figure 3. Room type distribution by host category. Takeaway: Larger hosts are more associated with entire-home rentals, making their activity look more commercial than casual.

Used casually or run like a business?

Availability provides one of the clearest behavioral signals in the dataset. Listings available only a small part of the year are more consistent with occasional hosting, while listings available most of the year look more like a continuous rental operation.

Figure 4. Distribution of listing availability over the next 365 days. Takeaway: The bimodal pattern suggests two distinct uses of Airbnb: occasional hosting and near year-round rental activity.
Figure 5. Availability by host category. Takeaway: Hosts with more listings tend to keep them available for larger parts of the year, a pattern that is consistent with more business-like use of the platform.

Where is Airbnb concentrated?

Geography adds the final piece. Airbnb is not spread evenly across New York City: Manhattan and Brooklyn dominate in sheer volume, and the concentration of larger hosts is not uniform either.

Figure 6. Number of Airbnb listings by borough. Takeaway: Airbnb activity is heavily concentrated in Manhattan and Brooklyn, so the platform’s effects are likely strongest there.
Figure 7. Host category composition by borough. Takeaway: Manhattan has a larger share of multi-listing hosts, suggesting that the platform is not only geographically concentrated but also unevenly professionalized.

What this analysis can and cannot show

These patterns do not directly prove that a host is a company, investor, or illegal operator. Rather, they show behavioral signals that are more or less consistent with casual home-sharing versus professional short-term rental activity.

In particular, availability does not mean actual occupancy, and a single snapshot cannot show how listings evolved over time. For that reason, the figures are best interpreted as evidence of a broader trend toward commercialization rather than definitive proof for every individual listing.

Final takeaway

Airbnb in New York City is best understood as a hybrid system.

Many hosts still look like occasional sharers, and private rooms remain an important part of the platform. But a smaller group of multi-listing hosts plays a disproportionately important role: they are more likely to operate entire homes, keep listings available for much of the year, and concentrate in central parts of the city.

In short: Airbnb in NYC no longer looks like a platform built only around casual sharing. It now combines home-sharing with a clearly more business-driven segment.
